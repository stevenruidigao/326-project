Overview

Our vision focuses on improving the learning experience of students across the world. Students possess a multitude of knowledge that can be exchanged, resulting in improved learning for everybody. Our web app aims to connect these varying pieces of knowledge. Specifically, we tailor to students who can help others with their classes in exchange for the same in return. Tutoring is often a one-way exchange of information in which only the tutee benefits. However, we want all parties involved to partake in learning and leave the session with knowledge that they deem useful and/or interesting. If you’re a student who needs help with CS 326 (just an example!), you can find a tutor who is willing to help but is looking to learn more about another area that you are familiar with. In that case, you’d both be a match and could tutor each other on the other’s desired subject. There’s no need for monetary exchange; the only exchange necessary is that of the mind.

Application Parts

Authentication is a primary component of our application. Since we want users (users are both tutors and tutees) to showcase what they can teach others and connect with them, they need a profile that contains that information such that other tutors can find them. Thus, a login is needed.

Profiles themselves are a critical component. For a user to be able to find someone who can teach them what they need to know, and to whom they can teach something in return, this information has to be available. Thus, profiles will contain a user’s “strengths” and “weaknesses” - or what they can teach others, and what they need to be taught/tutored on. To showcase the legitimacy of these users, there’ll be an XP system with ratings and comments that allow for others to provide feedback on their experience with said user.

Since the aim is to connect with someone so that both can expand their horizons, there also needs to be a method to communicate. Thus, a chat system that allows for inter-user communication is key. In addition to this chat, we’ll have a way to make requests – this will likely be how a chat gets opened between users. Then, they can communicate and establish what they can both gain from the swap.

Data Requirements

TutorSwap will need to handle authentication data, profile data, appointment data, and chat data to accomplish its goals. These four types of data will allow our application to function smoothly and properly.

Firstly, we need to store, manage, and securely use authentication data. We require the user to be logged in to interact with other users and make appointments, as well as to see the full profiles of other users. We may allow a logged-out user to view the number of tutors available in their area to convince them to sign up for an account, but we would also like to respect user privacy and require a login to prevent abuse through interactions. We will require the user to use an email address and password to log in and will use a session ID stored in the user’s browser to keep them logged in across pages. Users should not have access to other users’ non-public data such as email, and should only be able to edit their own data.

Secondly, one of the foundational data requirements for TutorSwap is each user’s profile data. This includes the basic profile information, such as the image for their profile picture, their name, and their unique username. We need to store tutoring-related information, such as courses/topics that they can tutor, topics/courses they want tutoring in, an XP number that represents their experience tutoring on this platform, rating data based on reviews from past tutees, and optionally a rough geographical location to more easily connect with other students in the same area. This information will allow students to easily see whether another student would be a good match for each other’s needs, as well as to see if they are generally good tutors.

Additionally, we need to store appointment data of past tutoring sessions, mainly so that we can store review data to be displayed on profiles and to help students gauge how good of a tutor another student may be. This would include storing a rating and comments for each review, the date of the tutoring session, whether the session was in-person or remote, and tags for the courses/topics covered in the tutoring session. Not all of this information will be public to everyone; we will only show the full date to the users involved in the appointment to preserve their privacy while allowing other users to gauge how recent this feedback is. This review data will allow students to hold other students accountable for their tutoring sessions, encouraging students to try their best when tutoring so that their good reviews will allow them to make more tutoring sessions in the future.

Finally, since we allow users to chat with each other to set up tutoring sessions, we need to record message data. This includes the users that each user is chatting with and the users that they’ve blocked. Additionally, we need to store chat logs, including the date and time of each message.

Storing the authentication, profile, appointment, and chat data is essential to ensuring that our web application functions properly. By storing this data on the server and only making the necessary bits available to users (depending on what they are able to see), we will be able to provide the functionality to allow skill exchanges.

Real World Connection

Our platform’s goal is to connect students to other students who have previous or current experiences in each other’s classes and are able to help each other. Our objective is to allow for students to get help with any issues they are confused about or generally receive tutoring to help them understand concepts better. In addition, we are trying to address the issue of how expensive tutoring can be. Looking online, it is easy to find tutors offering their services from $50/hr all the way to $200/hr, which isn’t very affordable and accessible to most college students. By allowing students to pay for tutoring with more tutoring, we are increasing the accessibility of tutoring to all students regardless of their financial means.

Finally, we are trying to address the issue of how difficult it can be to find and connect with others who have taken a specific class. Asking around the current class may not always be helpful, as many peers may also be confused or not understand the concepts well enough to teach them, and there is no easy way to find students who have taken the class in the past. We aim to solve this problem by offering students an easy and centralized way to look for help with a specific class or topic.

Integrative Experience

Firstly, our experiences in some of the courses from our university education directly relate to our ability to efficiently program this application using best practices. CS 220 has taught us many programming methodologies and best practices, which helps us write better and more efficient JavaScript for this web application. Those of us who have taken CS 345 have gained experience with managing and designing databases, which helps us better organize and manage user data in this web application.

Additionally, our experiences participating in many hackathons have given us lots of experience with developing full stack applications with an interactive and intuitive user interface. These experiences will help inform our decisions in crafting a beautiful user interface and a smooth user experience, as well as with managing user data in ways that are logical, organized, and secure.

Finally, our project idea is inspired by many of our struggles getting help in some of our classes. Even though many CS classes offered tutoring through the CICS tutoring portal in conjunction with CICS 298A, many of the General Education courses taught under different departments had different ways of offering tutoring and support that were sometimes difficult to navigate. Our goal with this application is to solve those issues by centralizing these tutoring resources in one site.
